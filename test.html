PractIV: Revolutionizing IV Training Through Realistic Simulation

Developing a next-generation intravenous training arm that better prepares healthcare professionals for real-world clinical challenges

The Challenge

Peripheral intravenous (IV) catheter insertion is one of the most common medical procedures performed in hospitals worldwide, yet first-attempt success rates among staff nurses can be as low as 44%. Failed attempts cause patient discomfort, treatment delays, and increased healthcare costs.

Current IV training models fall short in preparing clinicians for real-world challenges:

  • Oversimplified anatomy makes veins unrealistically easy to identify
  • Lack of tactile realism fails to replicate the feel of human tissue
  • Limited diversity with models representing only lean, light-skinned males
  • No complication simulation such as rolling veins or fragile tissue

These limitations mean that learners may become proficient in controlled environments yet remain underprepared for the variability and complexity of actual patient care.

Traditional IV training arms lack the realism needed for effective clinical preparation

Our Solution

Working in collaboration with the University of Washington's Engineering Innovation in Health program and the Center for Research and Education in Simulation Technologies (CREST), we developed PractIV: a modular, anatomically accurate IV training arm that addresses the critical gaps in current training tools.

Key Features

Anatomically Accurate Design

Based on the MoHSES™ female dataset from CREST with realistic vein placement from hand to upper arm

Realistic Tactile Feedback

3D-printed TPU lattice structures that mimic human soft tissue compliance

Modular Silicone Skin

Replaceable and adaptable for different patient scenarios

Pressurized Fluid System

5-15 mmHg pressure that provides blood flashback upon successful cannulation

Rolling Vein Simulation

Teaches proper stabilization techniques for difficult veins

Full-Body Integration

Compatible with MoHSES™ simulation systems for immersive scenario-based training

PractIV's modular design allows for realistic training across diverse patient scenarios

Development Process

User-Centered Design

We conducted extensive interviews and observational studies with nurses, clinicians, and trainees to understand the real challenges of IV insertion training. Through Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) , we mapped the decision-making and technical skills required for successful IV placement.

Iterative Prototyping

The development involved multiple iterations informed by direct clinical feedback:

  1. Early material testing to achieve realistic skin puncture forces (1.25-3.2 N)
  2. Lattice structure optimization using nTop software for tissue compliance
  3. Silicone formulation refinement (testing 4+ different recipes)
  4. Vein placement adjustments based on anatomical accuracy feedback
  5. Integration of pressurized fluid system for flashback simulation

Advanced Manufacturing

  • 3D-printed TPU lattice core using Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF)
  • Custom silicone casting with pigmented skin tones
  • Co-extruded silicone veins with blue outer layer for visibility
  • Modular assembly for easy maintenance and replacement

Validation & Results

We conducted a formal usability study with 8 participants (5 nurses, 1 surgeon, 2 simulation technicians) comparing PractIV against a leading commercial IV trainer.

System Usability

73.61 PractIV (Acceptable)
49.72 Commercial (Usability Concerns)

Anatomical Fidelity

Significantly higher ratings for PractIV:

  • Vein coloring realism: p = 0.0045
  • Skin tone realism: p = 0.024
  • Vein palpation fidelity: p = 0.031
  • Needle insertion feedback: p = 0.016
  • Soft tissue compliance: p = 0.0003

Educational Value

Participants rated PractIV significantly higher for:

  • Potential to train to professional competence: p = 0.043
  • Likelihood of curriculum adoption: p = 0.031

Clinical experts evaluating PractIV during usability testing

Impact

PractIV demonstrates that combining advanced manufacturing techniques with user-centered design can produce training tools that better prepare healthcare professionals for real-world challenges. The significantly higher ratings across realism, usability, and educational value suggest that this approach can improve both clinician confidence and patient outcomes.

Key Achievements

  • Validated improvement over commercial trainers through formal usability testing
  • Successfully integrated modular design for adaptability and maintenance
  • Demonstrated feasibility of 3D-printed compliant structures for medical simulation
  • Created foundation for future expansion (additional skin tones, anatomical variations, pathological conditions)

Next Steps

  • Training effectiveness studies measuring learner performance outcomes
  • Expansion of anatomical variations and patient scenarios
  • Integration into team-based, full-body simulation environments
  • Commercialization pathway development
